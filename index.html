Musical Family Tree

Hypotheses
H1Fans and artists make each other more valuable. Listeners come to discover; artists come to be found. The design gives fans reasons to return — following artists, getting updates on new releases and shows — while giving artists a reason to maintain an active presence.
H2People need a place to start. The current site drops visitors into a flat archive with no orientation. Curated entry points — by city or featured content — do more for new visitors than better search.
H3The family tree is the product. The connections between artists, bands, and projects are what make MFT worth visiting. Right now that's invisible. Making it navigable is the central design problem.
H4Getting on the platform has to be easy. If submitting means filling out a long form, fewer artists do it. Let them start with the minimum and add more over time. The archive grows when the barrier is low. Not addressed in this round.
H5Social traffic is growing — but only if the landing page delivers. Social is already the highest-engagement channel and grew 81% last period. Shareable song and artist pages turn that traffic into return visits. A post that lands on a page that doesn't deliver on what was promised loses people fast.
H6The artist page is a thread, not a destination. Shows, releases, and connections to other artists give visitors somewhere to go next — deeper into the MFT network rather than back to where they started.
designed by Mike Boutté · 2026
02 — Universal chrome

Player as nav host

The page body never carries navigation. In addition to traditional global navigation and search, map and network views attached to the global player UI provide the concept of Musical Family Tree as an interactive exploration mechanism.

Map answers where and when — geographic position across a time range. Network answers how — relational walking from any centered entity through shared members, labels, and credits. Both lenses are reached from icons inside the player so discovery is one tap from any page.

03 — Explorer

Map — geography

The map's job is geography (where things are). The network's job is connections (how things relate). Two surfaces, two jobs.

map vocabulary

Bands as filled circles — sized to suggest activity, not pinned to precise locations. Cities are meaningful positions. Venues as small outlined squares. The user's saves and follows render warmer (larger, darker fill) — the treatment whispers, doesn't shout.

period selector

Range slider with two handles at the bottom. Default: both at extremes (all time). Pulling to a constrained range filters to entities active in that period — the user moves through the decades of Indiana music geographically. The concentration of dots in a time and place tells its own story.

inset overlay

The explorer floats above the page the user came from. The underlying page blurs and dims; the panel has rounded corners and a soft shadow. The map is not a separate page — it's a lens opened from wherever the user is.

04 — Network

Walking the family tree

Always centered on a node. No cold/empty state. Tapping a connection recenters — not accumulates. The user walks, doesn't expand.

recentering, not accumulating

Four states walk through one path: open on a band → tap a member → tap one of that member's other bands → tap that band's label. Each step reframes the graph. The breadcrumb captures the trail; the back-edge is tappable to retreat. The previous center becomes a connected node marked "where you came from" — the path edge renders at 2× weight so the walk is visible at every step.

connection vocabulary
  • Member (current) — solid heavy line.
  • Member (previous) — dashed line with role + tenure label ("bass · 96–99").
  • Released on (label) — dot-dash to label squares.
  • Credits — thin solid to credited person nodes.
action chips

Four icon-only chips at compass points outside the focused node's ring: play (top), follow (right), view (bottom), share (left). Verbs adjust honestly per node type — venues can't be played; labels can't be followed the same way a band can.

bridge nodes carry meaning

Bands relate through members, labels, and credits. The user walks the family tree by tapping those bridge nodes — each hop through a person or label reveals the structural substance of the connection.

05 — Home page

How a user starts

Same surface for / and the user's home; no separate marketing-style page. Three states based on signal — page shape stays consistent, only the curated section varies.

rich state

A single curated song with reasoning. Album art at hero scale, centered. The play affordance attaches to the bottom edge of the art — symbolic, not button-shaped. The user reads the choice before the controls.

Reason copy as a centered caption beneath. Secondary actions ("try a different song," save) sit small underneath, not competing for attention.

thin / cold state

A swipeable card track of question forks: something popular, something near you, something nobody's heard, surprise me. The active card takes the visible width; adjacent cards visibly peek — clipped, not abstracted. Pagination dots beneath.

The forks aren't fixed: a user with a known location gets "near you" pointed at their city; without location, the system substitutes another fork.

activity feed

Sparse in cold/thin, dense in rich. Mixed event types in one stream — saves, plays, follows, shows announced, releases. Condensed prose, not boxes. Lowercase event labels with relative time. Label "recent" sits above the feed; no greeting, no "your activity" framing.

06 — Band page

The release identity

Answers "what did this release entity put out and who played in it." Identity hero → music → shows → members → connected bands → about → player.

members carousel

Circular thumbnails (people are circular). Each member's tile carries a small badge with a dot — dot size encodes connection count across MFT. A quiet signal that this person is a hub.

Tap a member → expands in place beneath the carousel to show that member's other bands and "also appears on" credits. Tap a connection to navigate; collapse to return.

connected bands

Vertical list with reasoning ("shares: member, label, city · 2 connections"). Connection-count dots on each row reflect density. The user sees why each band is connected before clicking.

submit a show

Any user can submit a past or upcoming show. Submission goes to the band's admin queue for approval. Past show submissions are how MFT recovers historical venue data.

earned connections only

Every connection on this page is structural — shared membership, shared label, documented credit. The data earns its place.

07 — Album page

Crate of releases

A focused state inside a band's catalog. The catalog is the parent surface; this album is one card on a horizontal track of releases. Adjacent cards visibly peek — the crate-flipping pattern.

header

Hamburger left, back affordance "← Band Name" right. The album page is a contextual child surface; the back affordance carries the band identity so the album hero doesn't have to.

inside the focused card
  • Hero — album art, title, year · type · runtime, linked label name. Save (heart) and share.
  • Tracklist — every song shown in full. Each row: number, play, title, save (heart), duration.
  • Liner notes — short editorial copy with a "Credits →" sub-affordance.
per-song save

Heart on every tracklist row, plus an album-level heart in the identity block. A user can save specific songs without saving the album, or save the album without hearting individual tracks.

08 — Person page

Band and People pages

Answers "what has this person done across the catalog." A pure session musician sees a different page shape than a label founder; someone who does both sees both.

system-generated summary

The hero subtitle ("Bloomington · drums, bass, label founder, producer") is composed from the data, not authored. What they've done says it.

about sits high

A person's story spans projects, so context comes first — short bio paragraph right after the identity hero, before bands and credits. Edit transparency and flag/suggest sit beneath.

composition
  • Bands — every band the person has been in, with role and tenure ("drums · current member").
  • Credits — production, engineering, guest spots, anything done outside their own bands.
  • Labels — labels the person founded, runs, or works for.
  • Frequent collaborators — circular thumbs of people this person has worked with most often, with a count. Earned through credits.
section adaptivity

Sections render only when there's content. The page shape reflects what the person has actually done.

parallel chrome

Person and band pages share the same header, player, and about/edit-transparency footer. The load-bearing middle differs by entity type — the system doesn't special-case solo artists; a person's band list simply includes their solo project like any other band.

09 — Label page

Label page

Answers "what did this institution put out." Less surface area than band or person pages — labels don't tour, don't carry credits the same way. Their job in the catalog is to be the institutional spine.

scale in the metadata line

A single-line subtitle combines location, founding year, and catalog scale: "Bloomington, IN · since 2002 · 38 bands · 124 releases." Density signals significance without a separate block — a glance tells you whether this is a micro-label or a regional institution.

roster · the load-bearing block

Bands/releases toggle. Bands view: thumbnail, name, era on the label, release count. Releases view: chronological list of every album/EP across all bands — for users who follow the label as a curator across artists.

default sort

Active (most releases first). With ~42 labels in the catalog, alphabetical wouldn't surface what matters — a handful are vastly more significant than the rest. Bands and people default to a-z; their catalogs are too large for "most active" to be meaningful at the top.

people

Horizontal carousel of circular thumbs — founder, co-founder, A&R. Each links to the person page; the network walk continues from here.

10 — Venue page

Venue page

Venues are nodes on the map but not in the network model — they're geographic, they don't form authorship structure. Upcoming shows lead; past shows read as a ledger.

composition
  • Upcoming — date stamp + linked bands; "+ submit" affordance for any user to add a show.
  • Regulars — carousel of square thumbs of the most-frequent acts, with show counts. For a venue with hundreds of shows, this surfaces the bands that defined the room.
  • Past shows — reverse-chronological ledger with date stamps and linked bands. Reads as a record of what happened here.
past shows recover history

Users submitting historical shows is the primary mechanism by which MFT recovers venue data that was never digitally captured. The "+ submit" affordance is not just for upcoming events — it's how the archive fills in what it missed.

network icon disabled

The only place in the system where the player chrome diverges. The icon stays in its spatial position (so the pattern doesn't break) but renders fully dimmed with no tap target. Communicates "not for this entity type" without removing the affordance from its place.

11 — Search

Universal navigation

Magnifying glass in the header on every page. Tap → fullscreen with input autofocused. For users who already know what they want.

cold state

Recent searches as small rows — each carries a magnifying glass icon (so they read as searches, not entities) and a × to remove. Below: Browse entry-points to wander the catalog by type — All bands, All people, All labels, Recently added.

New users see only the Browse section. Cold state is never empty.

active query

Flat ranked list of mixed-type results. Each row: entity image (square or circular per shape conventions), name, a small type badge ("band," "person," "venue," "album"), and a subtitle that explains the match.

Type badges are visually quiet — same weight as section labels elsewhere. A flat ranked list with badges reads cleaner than sectioning by type at typical query-result sizes.

behavior

Substring matching, flat ranked results. The catalog is small enough that this is tractable and readable without categorization overhead.

12 — Browse

Browse by type

Paginated lists of every entity of one type. Same compositional pattern across bands, people, labels, and recently added — only sort options vary.

defaults that make sense

Bands and people default to a-z. Their catalogs are large enough that "active" wouldn't surface what matters at the top. Labels default to active (most releases) since at ~42 entries, alphabetical wouldn't bring forward what's significant.

image shapes

Type-correct: square thumbnails for bands and labels, circular for people. The shape is a quiet legibility anchor — a glance tells you what kind of thing you're looking at.

A-Z scrubber

Visible on bands and people when alphabetical sort is active. Not on labels — the catalog is small enough that scroll is sufficient.

recently added

The only surface that mixes types. Each entry carries a small type badge alongside image, name, and subtitle — same pattern as search results. Filter chips at the top let the user narrow to one type.
